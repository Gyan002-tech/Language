The PREP Framework

A four-beat structure for answering on the spot without rambling.

The shape

P
Point — say your one-sentence answer first.
R
Reason — say why, in one sentence.
E
Example — give one concrete case, number, or story.
P
Point — restate the answer to close the loop.
Say the Point before you've fully justified it to yourself. The reason and example are what you build while you're already talking — this is what kills the pre-sentence pause.

Templates by context

Work meeting
P
"I think we should [decision]."
R
"because [the one factor that matters most]."
E
"For example, [a number, a past case, a specific scenario]."
P
"So — [decision], for that reason."
Everyday conversation
P
"Honestly, I think [opinion]."
R
"mainly because [personal reason]."
E
"Like, [a specific moment that shows it]."
P
"So yeah, [opinion], for me."
Debate / persuasion
P
"My position is [claim]."
R
"The key reason is [strongest single reason]."
E
"Consider [evidence, precedent, or a concrete case]."
P
"That's why [claim] is the right call."
Storytelling
P
"The short version: [what happened / the takeaway]."
R
"It happened because [the trigger or cause]."
E
"So here's what actually went down: [the story beat]."
P
"And that's why [takeaway] — that's the whole thing."

Note: PREP flattens most narratives into a single beat. For a longer story, treat the "E" as a mini-timeline (then / then / then) rather than one example.

Common failure modes

  • Reason-first: starting with "because..." before stating the point — listener has to hold an unresolved claim in their head.
  • No example: stops after Point + Reason — sounds like an assertion, not an argument.
  • Skipping the final Point: trails off after the example instead of closing the loop — this is often where fluency breaks down into filler words.
Source: Toastmasters International — "Impromptu Speaking". See also Lesson 0001.
