Lesson 0004 · Spoken Fluency & Articulation

Pick a word and keep going

Your doubt isn't really "I don't know English" — it's three specific, separate doubts. Each has its own fix.

Three doubts, not one

You named three flavors of doubt. They need different fixes, so it's worth separating them:

  • "Is this grammatically right here?" — usually not a grammar problem at all. It's a collocation problem: which words conventionally pair together.
  • "Is there a better word?" — a perfectionism problem. There's almost always a slightly better word — chasing it mid-sentence is what causes the pause.
  • "Do natives actually say this?" — a calibration problem, solved by checking after the conversation, not during it.

1. The "better word" doubt: the 3-second rule

Native speakers don't search for the perfect word in real time either — they use what SLA researchers call approximation: reaching for a close-enough word to keep the sentence moving, and refining later if it matters.

The rule: if a word (a) exists, (b) roughly means what you want, and (c) isn't obviously wrong — say it and keep going. Mentally flag it ("check that later"), but do not pause mid-sentence to search for a better one. The pause costs you more clarity than the imperfect word ever would.
This is a documented, named strategy — see Dörnyei & Scott (1997), "Communication Strategies in a Second Language," Language Learning, which lists "approximation" as a legitimate fluency strategy, not a shortcut for people who don't know the language.

2. The "is this grammatically right" doubt: it's usually collocation

English words pair up in fixed, sometimes arbitrary combinations. You can know every rule of grammar and still hesitate, because collocations aren't really about grammar — they're about convention. Cambridge Dictionary's collocation research shows these mistakes are extremely common even at advanced levels — it's not a sign you don't know English well.

Spot which phrasing is the one natives actually use. Both look grammatically plausible — that's the whole point.

3. The "do natives actually say this" doubt: check it after, not during

You cannot resolve this doubt mid-sentence — and trying to is exactly what breaks your fluency. Instead, build an after-the-fact habit: when a phrase nagged at you during the day, look it up that evening.
