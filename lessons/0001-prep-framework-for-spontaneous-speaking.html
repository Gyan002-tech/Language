Lesson 0001 · Spoken Fluency & Articulation

Stop the pause before you speak

A four-beat structure that turns "let me think..." into a complete, organized answer — on the first try.

The problem this solves

You said the gap is between understanding and producing: you know the answer, but by the time you open your mouth, the sentence hasn't been built yet — so it comes out clunky, or stalls into filler words.

The fix isn't "think faster." It's having a fixed shape to pour the answer into, so you only need to fill in content, not invent structure, while you're already talking.

The PREP framework

P
Point — say your one-sentence answer first, immediately.
R
Reason — say why, in one sentence.
E
Example — one concrete case, number, or moment.
P
Point — restate the answer to close the loop.
The trick is saying the Point first, before you've fully justified it in your head. Most clunky speech comes from trying to build the justification silently before speaking a word — PREP lets you think out loud instead, in a shape the listener can follow.

Before / after

Without structure
"Um, so, I mean, there's like the QA thing, and also the client asked for it early, so maybe — I don't know, it depends, but I guess if we — yeah, I think maybe we should wait, but I'm not totally sure."
With PREP
"I think we should push the launch back a week. QA found three bugs in the payment flow this morning. That's the same situation as last quarter, when we shipped early and had to hotfix twice in one week. So — pushing it back a week is the safer call."

Same content, same person, same amount of thinking. The only difference is the order it comes out in.

Check your ear for it

Each line below is one beat of a PREP answer. Identify which beat it is.

1. "So yeah, I think we should push the launch back a week."
2. "...because QA found three bugs in the payment flow this morning."
3. "Like last quarter, when we shipped early and had to hotfix twice in one week."
4. "So — pushing it back a week is the safer call."

Now build one yourself

Your manager asks in a meeting: "Should we hire a contractor, or wait and hire full-time?" Fill in one sentence per beat — don't overthink it, this is meant to feel almost too simple.

P: "I think we should hire a contractor first."
R: "because we don't know yet if this workload is permanent."
E: "Like when we hired full-time for the migration project, then had nothing for them six months later."
P: "So — contractor now, and we revisit full-time in a quarter."

Yours doesn't need to match this — the point is that you filled all four beats without stalling on any one of them. That's the actual skill.

Practice it out loud

Reading this is not the skill — saying it is. Pick one real question you might face this week (in a meeting, an argument, or a story you'd tell a friend) and say a PREP answer out loud, once, right now, even alone in a room. That single repetition is worth more than re-reading this page twice.
