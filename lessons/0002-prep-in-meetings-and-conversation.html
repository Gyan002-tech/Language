Lesson 0002 · Spoken Fluency & Articulation

PREP, applied: meetings & conversation

Same four beats, four different real situations — until the shape feels automatic, not borrowed.

Quick recap

P
Point — the answer, first.
R
Reason — why, one sentence.
E
Example — one concrete case.
P
Point — restate, close it.

Last lesson used one meeting example. That's not enough to feel like the shape works everywhere — so this lesson is nothing but worked examples: three from work meetings, three from everyday conversation. Read each pair, then say the "with PREP" version out loud before moving to the next.

Work meetings

1. Being asked for a status update

Without structure
"So, um, it's — mostly fine, I think? There were a couple of things, like the API thing took longer, but I mean overall it's — yeah, it should be okay, probably by end of week, unless something else comes up."
With PREP
"We're on track for end of week. The API integration took two extra days, but that was the only real blocker. For example, once that was fixed yesterday, the rest of the test suite passed clean. So — end of week is still the number."

2. Being asked to make a call under disagreement

Without structure
"I mean, I see both sides — Priya's point is valid, but also there's the deadline thing, so it's hard to say, maybe we could do both? I don't know, what does everyone else think?"
With PREP
"I'd go with Priya's approach. It costs us two extra days, but it avoids the rework we hit last time. For example, the caching bug in March cost us a full week because we skipped this exact step. So — two days now versus a week later, I'd take the two days."

3. Pushing back on a request from a manager

Without structure
"Yeah, I mean, we could try, it's just — there's a lot going on right now, so I'm not 100% sure, but I guess we'll see how it goes and let you know."
With PREP
"I don't think we can commit to Friday. The team is already covering two other launches this week. For example, Raj alone is on both the billing fix and the migration right now. So — Friday's too tight; Monday is realistic."

Everyday conversation

4. A friend asks your opinion on a decision they're facing

Without structure
"Honestly I don't know, it depends, like there are pros and cons to both, I guess it's just whatever feels right to you, you know?"
With PREP
"Honestly, I'd take the new job. Mainly because you've been unhappy with the commute for over a year now. Like, remember how drained you were after just the trial week last month? So yeah — I'd take it, for that reason alone."

5. Someone asks why you liked (or didn't like) something

Without structure
"It was fine, I don't know, some parts were good, some parts kind of dragged, I don't really have a strong opinion on it."
With PREP
"I actually didn't love it. Mainly because the pacing was really uneven. Like, the whole middle hour barely moved the story forward. So yeah — good idea, but it needed an editor."

6. Small talk that suddenly needs a real answer ("so what do you do?")

Without structure
"Oh, um, it's kind of hard to explain — I work in, like, software, sort of the backend side of things, it's a bit technical."
With PREP
"I build the backend systems for a scheduling app. Mainly the part that makes sure two people never get double-booked. Like, if you've ever used a booking site and it just works — that's the kind of problem I solve. So yeah, mostly invisible plumbing, but I like it."

What's the same across all six

  • The first sentence is always the answer — never a warm-up or a hedge.
  • The Example is always something specific and nameable — a date, a person, a past event — never "like, stuff happens."
  • The closing Point is short — often just the opening Point in fewer words, signposted with "so."

Match the example to the setting

Same PREP beat, two different settings. Which excerpt belongs to a work meeting, and which to a casual conversation? Look at word choice and tone, not content.

7. "So yeah — I'd take it, for that reason alone."
8. "So — two days now versus a week later, I'd take the two days."

Now build two of your own

One meeting scenario, one conversation scenario. Fill in real content — use something from this actual week if you can.

Meeting: your manager asks if the feature is ready to demo tomorrow

Conversation: a friend asks if you'd recommend your current gym/class/routine

Meeting:
"It's ready. The core flow is fully tested and stable. For example, it survived yesterday's full regression run with zero failures. So — good to go for tomorrow."

Conversation:
"Honestly, I'd say yes. Mainly because it's the first routine I've actually stuck with past a month. Like, I haven't missed a session in three weeks, which never happens for me. So yeah, I'd recommend it."

Say it, don't just write it

Say both of your answers out loud, right now, exactly as if the person had just asked you. The written version doesn't train the skill — the spoken one does. Notice which of the two settings felt more natural to say; that's useful signal for what to tell me.
